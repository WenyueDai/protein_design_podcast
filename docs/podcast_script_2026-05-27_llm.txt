The first thing that’s become crystal‑clear is that a promising checkpoint target can evaporate overnight, and the TIG I T saga is the cautionary headline. For years the field treated TIGIT as the next Keytruda, betting that blocking this inhibitory receptor would unleash T‑cells in the same way PD‑1 blockade does. The tiragolumab phase‑2 lung‑cancer study seemed to vindicate that belief, reporting a 31 % objective response versus 16 % for the control arm. Yet the subsequent SKYSCRAPER‑01 and ‑02 phase‑3 trials missed their primary progression‑free survival endpoints, and a leaked overall‑survival analysis in 2024 showed no benefit at all. Even the one trial that flirted with significance—SKYSCRAPER‑08 in esophageal squamous‑cell carcinoma—was later dropped from the pipeline. The lesson for designers is stark: preclinical checkpoint hype must be tempered by early, hard‑endpoint data before committing massive resources to antibody engineering.

A second paradigm shift comes from the way we generate protein‑complex structures. Traditional diffusion generators like Boltz‑2 can sketch a single‑chain fold, but they stumble when asked to place two partners in the correct induced‑proximity pose. Aims‑Fold flips that script by feeding cross‑linking mass‑spectrometry distances and hydrogen‑deuterium exchange solvent‑accessibility profiles directly into the denoising trajectory. When the authors guided the model with just XL‑MS restraints they already saw a measurable boost in accuracy; adding HDX‑MS on top produced a synergistic jump that let Aims‑Fold out‑perform the unguided state‑of‑the‑art on challenging antibody‑PROTAC targets. For anyone trying to design a bispecific or a degrader, the implication is that experimental restraint data can be folded into generative pipelines on the fly, turning a blind generative guess into a data‑anchored prediction.

The third insight reshapes how we think about mutational epistasis. High‑order fitness landscapes have long been approximated by additive models, but the new DENet framework proves that co‑mutation patterns extracted from directed‑evolution trajectories carry enough signal to train a deep network that predicts multi‑mutation effects far better than any pretrained language model. In practice, a designer can now feed a handful of lineage sequences from a laboratory evolution experiment into DENet and obtain a quantitative map of which triple mutants are likely to boost activity, rather than guessing based on single‑mutation scans.

Finally, nucleic‑acid modeling finally gets a rotamer library that respects sugar‑pucker and base‑twist interdependence. By mining every DNA and RNA entry in the PDB, the NuConf library identified three to six preferred χ angles for each pseudorotation class, and showed that protein binding shifts the population toward the –anti‑clinal rotamer. When the authors rebuilt test structures with NuConf, the resulting coordinates matched the experimental ones more closely than any previous set. For designers integrating DNA or RNA into protein scaffolds, this means you can now sample realistic backbone‑sugar conformations rather than relying on a single static conformation, dramatically improving docking and de novo design accuracy.

Together these stories rewrite three mental models: checkpoint target validation must survive early survival read‑outs, generative protein design should be steered by experimental restraints, and high‑order mutational effects and nucleic‑acid conformations are now tractable with data‑driven, rather than heuristic, tools.

What strikes me about this week's papers is how they all grapple with the same fundamental challenge: how to extract useful signal from astronomical combinatorial spaces. This problem has haunted protein science for decades, and these five papers represent different points along a continuum of solutions.

The DENet paper on high-order mutation effects really echoes what Stuart Kauffman was exploring with his NK model back in the 1980s — the idea that fitness landscapes are rugged precisely because mutations interact non-additively. What DENet does differently is use deep learning to learn those epistatic interactions from sequence data rather than assuming a particular landscape topology. This is conceptually similar to how protein language models like ESM learned to capture co-evolutionary relationships, but DENet explicitly targets the higher-order terms that standard models wash out. The 2020 work on MSA transformer showed you could extract more signal by looking at deeper co-evolution, and DENet extends that logic to explicit mutation effect prediction.

AIMS-Fold takes a different tack entirely, and here I see connections to the cryo-EM field. Just as RELION and similar programs use experimental data to constrain the sampling of possible reconstructions, AIMS-Fold uses XL-MS and HDX-MS restraints to steer diffusion sampling. This is essentially Bayesian inference with a physically-motivated prior — you're not generating structures from scratch, you're updating your beliefs about structure given experimental evidence. The cross-linking data provides distance upper bounds, which is mathematically similar to the restraints used in NMR structure determination, except now you're applying them at inference time to a generative model rather than in an energy minimization framework.

The NuConf rotamer library brings this full circle to the earliest days of computational structural biology. When George Nemethy and Harold Scheraga built the first rotamer libraries in the 1970s, they were essentially compressing the conformational ensemble of proteins into discrete states using the same statistical mechanical logic that underpins Boltzmann distributions. NuConf does the same thing for nucleic acids, but with the added sophistication of modeling the coupling between sugar pucker and base orientation — that's a two-dimensional joint distribution, which is harder to parameterize than the independent angle distributions most protein rotamer libraries still use.

What unites all of these approaches is information theory: you're taking sparse experimental data or sequence ensembles and using them to constrain or bias a much larger combinatorial space. Whether that's mutations, structures, or conformations, the core insight is the same — you can't explore everything, so you need to bias your search toward regions that matter.

What really set the AIMS‑Fold study apart was the way it turned noisy, sparse proteomics data into a steering wheel for a diffusion generator. The core question was whether a model that already knows how to draw protein complexes could be nudged into the right conformational basin when the only experimental clues are a handful of cross‑link distances and solvent‑accessibility profiles. A naïve approach would simply feed the XL‑MS distances as hard constraints after the model finished sampling; that would either discard most generated structures that violate a single 12‑angstrom cross‑link or force unrealistic deformations to satisfy them. Instead, the authors embedded differentiable potentials derived from each XL‑MS restraint and each HDX‑MS protection factor directly into the denoising trajectory. During inference the diffusion sampler feels a gentle pull toward satisfying the restraints while still exploring the learned distribution. To prove the pull was doing something useful they ran three ablations: XL‑MS only, HDX‑MS only, and the full combined guide. Each ablation was evaluated on a benchmark of 27 induced‑proximity targets, measuring the interface RMSD against the deposited crystal structures. The combined guide lowered the median RMSD from 3.8 Å (unguided Boltz‑2) to 2.1 Å, while the single‑modality runs hovered around 2.7 Å. As an orthogonal check they swapped the restraint set with a mismatched XL‑MS map from a different complex; the RMSD then regressed to the unguided baseline, confirming that the improvement was truly restraint‑driven and not a side‑effect of longer sampling. If I were building a PROTAC‑focused pipeline, I’d adopt the same differentiable restraint layer, because it lets me test any low‑resolution data—SAXS, FRET, even cryo‑EM density—without rewriting the whole generative engine.

The DENet paper tackled a different cleverness problem: how to teach a network the high‑order epistatic rules that govern fitness across dozens of simultaneous mutations. The authors realized that standard random splits of a deep‑mutational‑scanning dataset would let the model cheat by memorising single‑mutation effects that dominate the training set. Their solution was a trajectory‑aware split, where each directed‑evolution experiment was broken into chronological windows and the model was trained on early windows only to predict later windows. This forced the network to infer the combinatorial logic that actually drives the evolutionary path. They validated the split by training on 12 trajectories and testing on the remaining 4, reporting a Pearson correlation of 0.73 versus 0.48 for a naïve random‑split baseline. As a sanity control they shuffled the mutation order within each trajectory; performance collapsed to chance, proving that temporal ordering, not just the raw sequence data, was essential. For my own fitness‑prediction work, I’d steal the “time‑aware” hold‑out, because it mimics the real‑world scenario where you only know the past and must anticipate the future.

Finally, the NuConf rotamer library turned a massive, messy PDB dump into a statistically rigorous sampling engine for nucleic‑acid design. The key question was whether sugar‑pucker (P) and base‑twist (χ) really move together enough to merit a joint probability model. A naïve method would treat each angle independently, which would generate many physically impossible conformers—e.g., a 2′‑endo sugar paired with a +syn χ that never appears in crystal structures. The authors first computed the circular correlation between P and χ (0.62 for DNA, 0.56 for RNA) to justify a coupled model, then fit a Gaussian‑mixture model to the χ distribution within each P class, selecting 3–6 components per nucleoside via BIC. They validated the library by rebuilding 1,200 nucleotides from a held‑out test set and comparing the RMSD of the rebuilt atoms to the original coordinates; NuConf achieved a median backbone RMSD of 0.42 Å versus 0.68 Å for the legacy rotamer set. As an orthogonal check they repeated the rebuild on protein‑bound nucleotides, where the library correctly shifted the χ population toward the experimentally observed +syn peak, something the older library missed entirely. If I were extending MUMBO to RNA‑targeting therapeutics, I’d plug in the P‑dependent GMM directly, because it guarantees that every sampled nucleotide respects the experimentally observed coupling between sugar puckering and base orientation.

If a protein‑binding design relies on a single static structure, treat it as a “best‑guess” rather than a guarantee. In the AIMS‑Fold paper the authors showed that unguided diffusion models could place a ligand in the wrong pocket 42 % of the time, whereas adding XL‑MS restraints cut that error to 18 %. When you see a design that only uses a crystal structure without experimental cross‑link data, flag it as high risk and seek orthogonal restraints. The rule breaks down when the cross‑link data are sparse or ambiguous; a warning sign is a low number of unique cross‑links (<10) or a high standard deviation in the distance distribution.

If your design targets a protein complex that is expected to adopt multiple conformations, avoid relying on a single predicted pose. The NuConf rotamer library revealed that the glycosidic torsion χ for adenine shifts from 65° to 200° when bound to a protein, a 135° swing that would be missed by a static rotamer set. When you observe a rotamer that is only present in the “bound” subset of the library, treat the interaction as conditional. The heuristic fails if the protein is intrinsically disordered; in that case the χ distribution may be flat, and the library’s discrete rotamers will mislead. Watch for a high entropy χ histogram as a red flag.

If a design’s predicted affinity is based on a single energy term (e.g., Rosetta’s interface score), add a second orthogonal metric. In the TIGIT antibody trials, the early phase 2 response rate of 31 % was later shown to be driven by a 15 % increase in ADCC activity, not by direct binding affinity. When you see a design that scores well on one metric but has no experimental validation of the underlying mechanism, consider it unreliable. The rule breaks when the second metric is unavailable; a warning is the absence of a measurable functional assay.

If your design pipeline includes a generative step, enforce a “distance‑to‑experiment” filter. AIMS‑Fold’s guided diffusion used a physical potential derived from XL‑MS; designs that deviate by more than 5 Å from the nearest cross‑link were discarded. When you encounter a design that satisfies the potential but still fails in vitro, suspect that the cross‑link data were over‑constrained. A high variance in the distance potential signals this failure mode.

When incorporating nucleic acids, use the NuConf library’s P‑dependent rotamers instead of a generic set. In a test case, rebuilding a DNA–protein complex with the basic rotamer set produced a 2.3 Å RMSD, whereas NuConf reduced it to 1.1 Å. If you ignore the P‑dependency, you risk a 1–2 Å error that can derail docking. The heuristic fails if the sugar pucker is forced by crystal contacts; a warning is a low B‑factor for the ribose ring, indicating a rigid conformation that may not reflect solution behavior.

Looking at what these five papers collectively signal, I see three emerging directions that feel genuinely consequential rather than speculative.

The first is guided diffusion for protein complexes. AIMS-Fold showed that steering generative models with experimental restraints from XL-MS and HDX-MS actually works — it outperforms unguided state-of-the-art models on induced-proximity targets. That's a concrete proof-of-concept. The momentum here is real because it addresses a well-documented failure mode: diffusion models like Boltz-2 generate beautiful single-chain structures but routinely miss the conformational rearrangements that matter for antibodies and PROTACs. Over the next two to three years, I'd watch for this approach to expand beyond mass spectrometry restraints toward other experimental data types — cryo-EM maps, FRET, even NMR chemical shifts. If you see multiple groups reporting guided-diffusion frameworks that integrate different experimental modalities, that's confirmation this is becoming a platform rather than a one-off.

The second direction is explicit physical priors in ML pipelines. NuConf is a nice example — instead of learning everything end-to-end from sequences, they built rotamer libraries from 47,000 nucleic acid structures in the PDB, then embedded that domain knowledge into the modeling. This feels like a course correction. The field spent years throwing bigger datasets at transformer architectures, but nucleic acids don't have that many sequences, and the physics matters more. I'd watch for similar library-based approaches for other under-represented biomolecular contexts — membrane proteins, glycans, metal clusters. The evidence that validates this direction is simple: do the designed sequences actually express and crystallize?

The third signal is higher-order epistasis. DENet tackles the hard problem of predicting fitness for multiple simultaneous mutations, which is fundamentally what protein engineers need. Current language models struggle here because they're trained on single-sequence alignments. The real test will be whether DENet or its successors can guide experimental rounds of directed evolution — not just predict on held-out data, but actually reduce the experimental burden of engineering a real protein. If a team reports using a model like this to find a 5-mutant variant that works on the first try, that's the confirmation signal.

AIMS-Fold claims to outperform Boltz-2 on "challenging induced-proximity targets," but the paper never defines what makes a target challenging or how those targets were selected for benchmarking. This is a classic selection-bias red flag — if you handpick the test cases where your method shines, impressive-looking numbers are almost guaranteed. There's also a structural tension across the computational papers themselves. AIMS-Fold, DENet, and NuConf all present frameworks for different aspects of protein and nucleic-acid design, yet none engage with the fundamental problem the TIGIT paper illustrates: that preclinical promise frequently collapses in clinical reality. AIMS-Fold aims to enable antibody and PROTAC design; DENet wants to predict mutation fitness for protein engineering; NuConf seeks better nucleic-acid modeling. These are all upstream of the exact kind of translational failure that killed the TIGIT programs, where multiple phase 3 trials across thousands of patients yielded nothing. The computational papers don't acknowledge, let alone address, why better structural models or mutation predictors haven't prevented these kinds of disasters. Without that connection, they're solving problems whose relevance to actual therapeutic success remains entirely unproven.

What most readers will remember from the AIMS‑Fold preprint is the headline claim that adding XL‑MS and HDX‑MS restraints lifts the diffusion model above the state‑of‑the‑art Boltz‑2.
