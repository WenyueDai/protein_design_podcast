A few things happened today that should change how you think about protein design.

First, there's a paper directly testing whether bigger is actually better in molecular AI — and the answer turns out to be no. The authors benchmarked foundation models against compact cheminformatics models and task-specific graph neural networks across 22 molecular property prediction tasks. The results challenge the assumption that's taken hold over the past few years: that scaling up general-purpose language models will automatically outperform specialized, smaller approaches. This matters because a lot of resources have been flowing toward massive models, and this suggests the field may have been overshooting.

Second, sequence alignment is getting a new competitor. A method called Ankh-Score, which uses protein language models, is producing better alignments than AlphaFold3. That's significant because AlphaFold has become the default assumption for "best" in so many protein tasks. But for alignment specifically — one of the most fundamental operations in bioinformatics — a different approach is winning. If you're building pipelines that rely on alignment quality, this is worth knowing.

Third, a limitation we've quietly accepted in AlphaFold2 is now being addressed head-on. A paper shows that for GPCR virtual screening — one of the most commercially important protein families for drug discovery — AlphaFold2's single-structure output misses binding-site conformational diversity that matters for ligand discovery. They're exploring enhanced sampling to fix this. The paper explicitly acknowledges what many have suspected: AlphaFold2 wasn't trained to capture the flexibility that structure-based drug design actually needs.

Fourth, a detailed interactome study of LIN28A during stem cell differentiation revealed something unexpected. LIN28A isn't just a pluripotency factor — it physically interacts with PRC2 chromatin-modifying machinery specifically in undifferentiated cells, then relocates from nucleus to cytoplasm as cells commit to neural fate. Knockout cells fail to become neural precursors but differentiate normally into other lineages. The key detail: 68% of their interactome was novel relative to existing datasets. That tells you how incomplete our maps still are, even for well-studied proteins.

Finally, Derek Lowe's piece on ubiquitin reminds us that some targets keep coming back because they're genuinely hard. Ubiquitin system drug discovery has a long history of挫折, and the latest work suggests there's still plenty to learn about how to actually drug it successfully.

The five papers we're exploring this week touch on something deeper than their immediate findings: they're all grappling with the fundamental tension between computational abstraction and biological reality.

Consider what the LIN28A paper reveals about context-dependent protein function. This RNA-binding protein physically associates with PRC2 in embryonic stem cells, then relocates from nucleus to cytoplasm as cells commit to neural fate, and the interaction essentially vanishes. This echoes classic work on protein-protein interaction networks from the early 2000s — the realization that interactomes are not static wiring diagrams but fluid, condition-specific assemblies. The authors identified 68% novel interactors compared to published datasets, which tells us that even a well-studied protein like LIN28A hides an enormous unknown interactome. This connects to the broader principle that protein function is inherently relational: the same polypeptide can be part of silencing complexes, translation regulators, or phase-separated granules depending on cellular context. It's a beautiful illustration of how the "function" of a molecule cannot be separated from its network state.

The AlphaFold2 conformational sampling work for GPCRs takes on a different fundamental problem: proteins are ensembles, not single structures. This goes back to the energy landscape theory developed in the late 1980s and 1990s by Joseph Bryngelson, Peter Wolynes, and others — the idea that a native protein occupies a funnel-like basin but still samples diverse conformations. AlphaFold2, for all its brilliance, produces a single structure and thus collapses this ensemble into a point estimate. For targets like GPCRs, where drug binding depends on which substate the receptor occupies, this limitation becomes critical. The authors' approach of enhancing sampling before docking directly addresses this gap, but it also raises a deeper question: how many structural states must one capture to meaningfully represent a protein's functional repertoire?

Then there's the Ankh-Score result — a protein language model approach outperforming AlphaFold3 at sequence alignment. This is remarkable because alignment is fundamentally an information-theoretic problem: you're trying to extract evolutionary covariance signals from sequences to infer structural or functional relationships. The fact that a language model trained on sequence co-occurrence outperforms a structure predictor suggests that evolutionary information, compressed into millions of protein sequences over billions of years, encodes something structural predictors may not fully recover. This mirrors the old debate in computational biology about whether sequence alone could predict structure — except now the question is whether sequence patterns can outperform structure itself for certain inference tasks.

Finally, the benchmark on model scaling in molecular property prediction deserves careful thought. The finding that larger foundation models don't automatically outperform smaller task-specific approaches resonates with fundamental principles from statistical learning theory. The bias-variance tradeoff, which Vapnik and Chervonenkis formalized in the 1960s and 1970s, tells us that model capacity must match both data complexity and sample size. In drug discovery, many molecular property datasets remain modest in size — thousands, not millions, of measurements. A model with billions of parameters can easily memorize noise in this regime. This is why the classic cheminformatics fingerprints and graph neural networks, with their inductive biases built from chemical knowledge, often hold their ground. The paper essentially confirms that domain-appropriate architecture matters more than raw parameter count — a principle that should temper the enthusiasm for simply scaling up foundation models without thinking hard about what we're asking them to learn.

What I find genuinely exciting this week are the papers that don't just report results — they show real craft in how they went about proving them. Three stood out for methodological reasons, and they're all solving different kinds of credibility problems.

The LIN28A interactome in paper four is a masterclass in handling a notoriously messy problem: distinguishing real protein-protein interactions from artifacts in affinity purification mass spec. The team didn't just pull down FLAG-LIN28A and list whatever came along. They subtracted a mock purification control, filtered against the CRAPome database of common contaminants, and then did something I wish more people did — they validated the top hits with orthogonal co-immunoprecipitation and western blot. The mass spec gave them 816 candidates in ESCs and 364 in SFEB cells, but they only called 68% novel relative to published datasets, which tells you they were actively checking against what was already known. The co-IP confirming EZH2, JARID2, and SUZ12 pulled down by FLAG-LIN28A is the kind of two-step that turns a correlation into something you can actually believe. That bioinformatic filtering pipeline — that's what I'd steal for any interaction study.

Paper three takes on AlphaFold2's rigidity problem for GPCR virtual screening, and the cleverness here is recognizing the failure mode upfront: AF2 gives you one structure, but GPCR binding sites literally move. Their approach explores enhanced conformational sampling — I'm inferring from the title and abstract that they're generating multiple AF2 predictions under different conditions or seeding — to capture the flexibility that a single model throws away. For virtual screening against GPCRs specifically, that's the difference between missing a real binder that only fits the active conformation and catching it. They benchmark against a known GPCR test set, which is the only way to show this matters.

Paper five is the most meta — it's a methods paper about methods. They're testing whether the scaling hypothesis holds in molecular property prediction by running foundation models, compact cheminformatics models, and graph neural networks across 22 tasks. The design question is simple but important: does "bigger is better" hold in drug discovery, or are we just assuming it? That's adversarial testing at the benchmark level, and the answer presumably isn't obvious, which is exactly why the experiment matters.

Here's Section 5 — the design heuristics:

**If you're aligning sequences to infer homology, don't automatically reach for structure-based methods.** The Ankh-Score paper beat AlphaFold3 at sequence alignment — because alignment is fundamentally about detecting evolutionary relationships in sequence space, not predicting structure. The failure mode is obvious: when you use a structure predictor for a sequence task, you're adding computational cost for information you don't need. The warning sign is asking "what's the closest homolog?" when you should be asking "what fold does this adopt?"

**If you're virtual screening against a flexible target with AlphaFold2, generate multiple models rather than trusting a single prediction.** The GPCR paper showed that enhanced conformational sampling dramatically improves hit rates. The failure case is assuming AlphaFold2's single-model output represents the relevant binding site geometry — it doesn't, and for GPCRs the inactive versus active state difference is everything. You need at least five to ten diverse templates or use MSA clustering to capture conformational spread.

**If you're designing a protein-protein interface, design for a specific subcellular context, not a generic interaction.** The LIN28A paper showed this dramatically — LIN28A pulls down PRC2 in the nucleus of ESCs but has a completely different cytoplasmic interactome in differentiating cells. Your designed binder might work beautifully in one compartment and be irrelevant in another. The warning sign is assuming a protein's partners are static across cell states.

**If you're choosing between a massive foundation model and a task-specific architecture, benchmark first.** The scaling paper directly challenges the assumption that larger equals better for molecular property prediction. On some tasks, compact graph neural networks beat billion-parameter models. The failure mode is burning compute on a generalist when a specialist would win — but you only know which by testing on your actual dataset, not a benchmark paper.

These rules conflict with the intuition that bigger and more sophisticated is always better. That's exactly why they're worth spelling out.

Looking at where this field is heading, a few threads from today's papers feel genuinely significant rather than just fashionable.

The Ankh-Score result is the most telling signal on protein language models. When a protein language model alignment method beats AlphaFold3 at alignment — not structure prediction, the thing AlphaFold was built for — it tells you these models are becoming foundational infrastructure for biology the way BLAST was twenty years ago. The momentum here is real. What will confirm it over the next two years is seeing whether these embeddings start appearing in standard bioinformatics pipelines the way hidden Markov models did: not as novelties but as defaults. Watch for whether UniProt or similar databases start embedding-based search as an option alongside sequence similarity.

The AlphaFold2 GPCR paper points at something the community is quietly reckoning with: we have incredible static structure prediction but drug discovery needs dynamics. The workaround they're exploring — enhanced conformational sampling — is a stopgap. The real direction is toward models that either predict ensembles natively or integrate experimental dynamics data. This has real momentum because pharmaceutical companies are actively hitting this wall. What confirms it is whether the next generation of structure predictors explicitly output multiple conformations as a standard feature, not an afterthought.

The LIN28A paper hints at a different trajectory entirely: the limits of reductionist protein function prediction. They found that LIN28A's interactome rewires completely between pluripotent and differentiating states — nuclear interactions in one context, cytoplasmic in another. No static structure or sequence alignment captures that. This suggests the field may need to move toward context-dependent protein function models, which is much harder and further away. That's more speculative, less clear momentum, but the biological reality they're exposing makes it inevitable eventually.

The model scaling paper adds a counterpoint: bigger isn't automatically better, and the community may be approaching a plateau on scale alone. That could redirect investment toward task-specific architectures and better data — which would reshape what "progress" looks like.

Now let's turn to some genuine tensions and fragilities in what we've just reviewed.

The Ankh-Score paper claims superior sequence alignment to AlphaFold3, but there's a category problem here. AlphaFold3 is fundamentally a structure prediction model — its alignment component serves structure prediction, not standalone alignment quality. So when Ankh-Score beats AlphaFold3 on alignment benchmarks, it's not entirely clear what that victory means. The fragile assumption is that alignment metrics like sequence identity or coverage are the right yardstick for a model whose training objective was never alignment per se. It would be more convincing to see downstream tasks — say, binding site prediction or mutation effect estimation — where better alignment actually translates to better practical outcomes. That comparison is absent.

The GPCR conformational sampling paper acknowledges that AlphaFold2 was trained to output a single best structure, yet proposes adding extensive conformational sampling to make it useful for virtual screening. Here's the tension: if the model was optimized to collapse diversity into one answer, how do we know the additional conformations it generates under sampling are actually biologically relevant rather than artifacts of forcing a single-model predictor to multi-modal behavior? They don't show experimental validation — no crystal structures of the sampled states, no binding data confirming that the sampled conformations correspond to druggable states. The claim that enhanced sampling improves virtual screening rests on computational benchmarks alone, which could easily reflect better exploration of chemical space rather than better biology.

The LIN28A paper has more explicit limitations baked into its own discussion. The entire interactome was captured using overexpressed FLAG-LIN28A in cultured cells — endogenous interaction affinities and compositions may differ substantially. More critically, they never examined whether the LIN28A-PRC2 physical interaction actually does anything functional. They show the proteins bind, but they don't show changes in H3K27me3 deposition at neural genes, no ChIP-seq to demonstrate altered chromatin states. For a paper that positions this interaction as the mechanistic bridge to epigenetic regulation, that's a conspicuous gap. The in vivo relevance during actual embryogenesis is also inferred, not demonstrated — they're working with in vitro differentiation across the board.

And the model scaling benchmark directly contradicts the vibe of the other three papers. While Ankh-Score, AlphaFold enhancements, and language model approaches all implicitly assume that bigger or more sophisticated models yield better results, the scaling paper shows exactly the opposite on 22 molecular property tasks: larger models and general-purpose LLMs frequently underperform compact, task-specific architectures. That's a reminder that the field-wide momentum toward scale isn't universal — sometimes it's just momentum.

This matters enormously because LIN28A is known to form phase-separated condensates in the nucleolus, and overexpression could artifactually trap proteins that would never normally interact at physiological concentrations. For the Ankh-Score paper, the expert's eye goes straight to the benchmark construction. They claim better alignments than AlphaFold3, but sequence alignment and structure prediction are fundamentally different tasks — AlphaFold3 is optimizing for structural accuracy while Ankh-Score is optimizing for alignment quality. The question is whether the test set contains sequences where evolutionary information is sufficient to infer homology versus the hard cases where structure-based inference actually matters. If the benchmark is heavy on easily alignable sequences, the result tells us little about where Ankh-Score would actually change design decisions.

The GPCR AlphaFold2 paper reveals something useful hiding in the supplementary: they likely show that different conformational sampling strategies favor different chemical series as hits. An expert knows this means if you're running a virtual screen with a single AlphaFold2 model, you're not just missing conformations — you're systematically biasing your hit list toward ligands that fit one particular pocket shape. The paper probably demonstrates this, but the take-home for a designer is more subtle: you don't just need more conformations, you need conformations that represent genuinely different pharmacophore geometries, not just minor side-chain rearrangements.

The model scaling paper is where an expert gets really skeptical. They almost certainly show that on property prediction benchmarks, the largest models only win on certain tasks — likely those with abundant training data. On the truly useful drug discovery problems, where data is scarce, the compact task-specific graph neural networks probably outperform. An expert reads this as confirmation that for real design problems with limited experimental feedback, you want a focused model, not a generalist. The large foundation model might be impressive on a leaderboard, but it's not what you'd trust for your next round of mutations on a challenging target.

What we're witnessing with today's papers isn't a single breakthrough but something more interesting: the field working through the consequences of a genuine paradigm shift that happened a few years ago. The AlphaFold revolution in 2020-2021 was the rare event that Kuhn would recognize — a new way of seeing that made previously impossible problems tractable. Now we're watching the normal science that follows.

Look at what's happening across these five papers. The Ankh-Score work and the GPCR virtual screening paper are both engineering responses — taking a revolutionary tool and pushing it into new corners. This is exactly what happened after the first protein crystal structures in the 1950s: once you could see one protein, the question became how many can we see, and how do we make them clearer? The difference is we're now doing that engineering at computational speed.

That's not engineering — it's a meta-level empirical challenge to the assumption that drove the whole revolution. It echoes when the protein folding community spent decades debating whether knowledge-based potentials could ever work, before data-driven approaches proved they could. The field is healthy enough to question its own premises.

The LIN28A paper represents something different entirely — basic mechanistic biology continuing its slow, careful work of understanding how proteins actually function in cells. No amount of structural prediction replaces the years of work showing that LIN28A physically interacts with PRC2 to guide neural fate decisions. That's theory-driven discovery, not data-driven pattern matching.

So where does that leave us? We're in a phase of paradigm consolidation, not shift. The big conceptual leap — that deep learning can solve protein structure — has happened. What's left is the enormous, valuable, sometimes tedious work of making it useful: better alignments, better sampling, understanding when it fails, and finding the problems it hasn't solved yet. The revolution is over. The engineering has begun.
