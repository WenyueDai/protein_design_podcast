Molecular crystal structure prediction is one of those problems that sits at the intersection of chemistry, physics, and computational science. The challenge is that molecules in crystals aren't just randomly arranged—they're held together by a delicate balance of weak intermolecular forces like van der Waals interactions and hydrogen bonds. These forces are subtle, and the possible arrangements of molecules in a crystal lattice explode combinatorially as the size of the molecule increases. Traditional methods like density functional theory are accurate but computationally expensive, making exhaustive searches impractical for large systems.

The paper we're looking at today, MolCrystalFlow, takes a different approach. Instead of brute-force searching, it uses a generative model based on flow matching—a technique that's been successful in other domains but hasn't been widely applied to crystal structure prediction. The key idea is to train a model to learn the distribution of stable crystal structures directly from data, so it can generate new, plausible structures without having to evaluate every possible arrangement.

MolCrystalFlow builds on the CrystalFlow architecture, which was originally designed for inorganic crystals. The authors adapted it for molecular crystals by incorporating a new way to represent molecules—using a combination of atom-level and molecule-level features. This allows the model to capture both the internal structure of the molecule and how it interacts with its neighbors in the crystal. They also introduced a symmetry-aware loss function, which helps the model respect the periodic nature of crystals.

One of the most interesting aspects is how they handle the generation process. Instead of generating the entire crystal at once, they use a sequential approach: first placing molecules in the lattice, then refining their positions and orientations. This is guided by a learned score function, which tells the model how to adjust the structure to make it more stable.

The results are promising. On a benchmark dataset of molecular crystals, MolCrystalFlow achieved a 24% success rate in generating structures within 1% energy of the ground truth—significantly better than previous methods. It also scales well to larger molecules, which is a major advantage over traditional approaches.

There are still limitations. The model requires a dataset of known crystal structures for training, so it can't discover entirely new types of packing. And while it's much faster than DFT-based methods, it's still slower than some heuristic approaches. But for many applications—like drug discovery or materials design—the accuracy and scalability of MolCrystalFlow could make it a valuable tool.

In summary, MolCrystalFlow represents a significant step forward in molecular crystal structure prediction. By combining generative modeling with domain-specific insights, it offers a new way to tackle a problem that's been stubbornly difficult for decades.

Mixture of Experts models have been a breakthrough for scaling large language models efficiently, but their internal workings have remained somewhat mysterious. A new analysis from arXiv cs.LG reveals something surprising: in these models, a small number of specialized experts handle most of the work.

The researchers examined DeepSeekMoE, which uses 64 routed experts with 6 active per layer. What they found was striking. Across different specialized domains, very few experts handle over half of all routing decisions. This means the model's "expertise" is highly concentrated rather than evenly distributed.

To understand why this matters, consider how MoE models work. Instead of activating all parameters for every input, they route tokens to specific expert networks. The routing mechanism learns which expert is best for which type of input. The analysis showed these routing patterns are far from random - certain experts become specialists for certain types of content.

The team developed an "early decoding framework" to track how individual experts contribute to final outputs. By examining hidden states at intermediate layers, they could measure how much each expert's output influenced the final representation. The results were revealing: the hidden state similarity between using a single top-weighted expert versus the full ensemble reached up to 0.95 in some layers. That's remarkably high - it means one expert's output closely approximates what the full group produces.

This concentration of expertise has practical implications. When the researchers tested using only a single expert across all domains, model perplexity increased by just 5%. For context, perplexity measures how well a language model predicts text - lower is better. A 5% increase while using far fewer parameters represents a significant efficiency gain.

The findings suggest MoE models could be optimized by identifying and pruning less-critical experts, potentially reducing memory and computation costs substantially without major performance loss. It also provides new insights into how knowledge is organized within these architectures - not as a distributed network, but as a collection of specialists where a few handle most of the workload.
