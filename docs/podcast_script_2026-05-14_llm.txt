The way we think about protein language models is fundamentally shifting. For years, we've treated them as black boxes — powerful but opaque, predicting function and structure without any real handle on why they work. ProtoMech changes that calculus entirely. When the researchers applied cross-layer transcoders to ESM2, they recovered 82 to 89 percent of the model's original performance on protein family classification and function prediction tasks. That's remarkable on its own, but here's what really matters: they found compressed circuits using less than one percent of the latent space that still retained 79 percent of the accuracy. And when they used these circuits to steer protein design, they beat baseline methods in over 70 percent of cases. The implication is stark — we don't need to understand the entire massive model to engineer proteins. Specific computational circuits correspond to specific structural and functional motifs, and targeting those circuits directly is more efficient than brute-force fine-tuning.

This connects directly to what Ensembits is doing with protein structure tokenizers. The field has been building language models on static PDB structures, treating proteins as single conformations. Ensembits is the first tokenizer designed for conformational ensembles — it learns geometric descriptors across multiple states. When they tested it, it outperformed prior methods on root-mean-square fluctuation prediction, which is essentially predicting flexibility. Even more striking, it matched or exceeded static tokenizers on functional tasks like enzyme commission classification, Gene Ontology prediction, and binding site prediction — despite using far less pretraining data. A single predicted structure can now generate dynamics tokens that capture how proteins move, not just what they look like in one snapshot. The thinking here is fundamentally different: dynamics aren't noise to be averaged away, they're information that's more informative per data point than static structure alone.

On the experimental side, antibody quality assessment has been stuck measuring ensemble averages — you get one number for the whole population, losing the outliers that matter most. A new method using single protein oscillators can measure size and charge simultaneously on individual molecules, giving single-molecule resolution instead of population averages. This matters because aggregation reduces therapeutic effectiveness and increases immunogenicity, and you can't catch problematic subpopulations when you're only looking at the mean.

In a completely different direction, viral spillover prediction has traditionally relied on ecological and host traits — where does the virus live, what does it infect. A new approach uses machine learning on viral protein sequences alone to predict which viruses might jump hosts. The field is discovering that the viral sequence itself contains information about host range that we didn't know to look for.

These aren't incremental improvements. They're changing what questions we think are even answerable.

The two most substantive papers today touch on ideas that have deep roots in protein physics and machine learning theory, and seeing those connections is genuinely exciting.

ENSEMBITS tackles protein conformational ensembles, which brings us back to the energy landscape perspective that Bryngelson, Wolynes, and Onuchic developed in the late 1980s and 1990s. The core insight from that era was that proteins aren't static structures but dynamic populations sampling many states, each with its own free energy. RMSF — the metric ENSEMBITS uses to benchmark — is essentially a thermodynamic expectation value over that ensemble. What makes ENSEMBITS clever is its frame-distillation objective, which distills information across multiple conformations into a compact representation. This echoes the maximum entropy approach to protein dynamics that NMR spectroscopists have used for decades: you want the simplest model that reproduces the observed distribution of states. The fact that ENSEMBITS can predict dynamics tokens from a single predicted structure is particularly striking — it's like inferring the ensemble from one snapshot, which is the inverse problem that has haunted protein folding theory since Anfinsen's dogma.

ProtoMech takes a different but equally profound angle: treating protein language models as systems whose internal computations can be dissected. This is directly inspired by the mechanistic interpretability movement that grew out of work by Chris Olah and collaborators at Anthropic, who showed that neural networks develop interpretable features in their middle layers. The lottery ticket hypothesis from Frankle and Carlin — the idea that dense networks contain sparse sub-networks that do most of the heavy lifting — finds a parallel here: ProtoMech identifies compressed circuits using less than 1% of the latent space that retain 79% of the model's accuracy. That's remarkably efficient, and it suggests that ESM2, like other large language models, may be relying on a relatively small set of high-leverage computational motifs to encode evolutionary information about protein structure and function. The steering experiments — where the researchers nudge the model along these circuits to generate higher-fitness proteins — connect to recent work on representation engineering, where targeted interventions in latent space can shift model behavior in predictable ways. This is beginning to feel less like black-box optimization and more like true mechanistic control.

The most methodologically clever moves in this batch come from the ENSEMBITS paper and the ProtoMech framework, and they tackle completely different kinds of problems.

ENSEMBITS faced a fundamental problem: how do you tokenize a protein ensemble when different conformations have different numbers of atoms, and the ordering is arbitrary? A naive approach would just concatenate frames and hope the model figures it out, but that creates massive data sparsity and breaks permutation symmetry. Their solution was elegant: they trained a Residual Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder with a frame-distillation objective that essentially learns a fixed-size vocabulary of geometric descriptors across multiple conformations, then encodes variable-size ensembles in a permutation-invariant way. The real cleverness is in the downstream validation — they didn't just claim it works on dynamics prediction, they showed it can predict dynamics tokens from a single predicted structure. That's an extremely hard stress test: take one static AlphaFold prediction, generate the full ensemble token sequence, and see if downstream tasks still work. Most approaches would collapse there because they overfit to the training distributions of multiple conformations.

ProtoMech took a different angle entirely. The question was: can you recover the computational circuitry of a 15-billion-parameter protein language model without training a separate model from scratch? Their cross-layer transcoder learns sparse latent representations jointly across all 36 layers of ESM2, which is computationally expensive but necessary — prior work treated each layer independently and missed the cross-layer interactions that actually implement the model's reasoning. The adversarial test here is striking: they compressed the circuit to less than 1% of the latent space and still retained 79% of the accuracy. That's a brutal ablation. If the circuits were artifacts, they'd vanish under that compression. Instead, the latent features corresponded to real structural motifs like binding sites and stability regions, and when they steered the model along these circuits for protein design, they beat baseline methods in over 70% of cases. The fact that steering a compressed 1% circuit outperforms the full model in some cases is the kind of result that makes you sit up and take notice.

Here's a heuristic that jumps out from the ProtoMech paper and directly changes how you should think about protein design: if you're using a large protein language model as a black box, you're leaving massive efficiency on the table. The paper showed that compressed circuits using less than one percent of the latent space still retained 79 percent of the model's accuracy on functional tasks. That means for any specific design objective, you probably don't need the full model — you need to find the right circuit and steer along it.

Concretely, if you're designing binders against a target and your first round hits something in the high nanomolar range, don't just iterate blindly on that sequence. Instead, ask whether you've identified the right functional circuit in your model. ProtoMech found that steering along discovered circuits beat baseline methods in over 70 percent of cases. The warning sign that you're in a failure case is when your designs consistently hit the same affinity ceiling regardless of sequence changes — that suggests you're not accessing the high-fitness circuit, just sampling noise around it.

Here's the second rule from Ensembits: if your design requires conformational flexibility — and most antibody-antigen interactions do — then tokenizing only the static structure is actively misleading you. The paper demonstrated that even starting from a single predicted structure, you can predict dynamics tokens that capture correlated motions. The failure mode is obvious: designs that look perfect in a crystal structure context but fail in solution or under binding stress. If you're seeing that pattern, go back and model the ensemble, not just the mean structure.

One more practical note from the antibody aggregation paper, which applies to any therapeutic protein work: if you're only measuring size, you're missing charge heterogeneity that predicts stability. The paper showed simultaneous size and charge measurement caught aggregation-prone species that size alone would miss. If your developability assays only include SEC, add charge heterogeneity to your workflow — it's a negative filter that catches failures before they happen in vivo.

ENSEMBITS is the first tokenizer explicitly built for conformational ensembles, and it actually outperforms static methods on flexibility prediction while matching them on functional tasks, despite using far less training data. That's a strong signal. The field spent five years solving static structure with AlphaFold2; now multiple groups are racing toward dynamics. The momentum here is real because it's no longer just "look, attention heads match structural features." What I'd watch for: whether other groups replicate this with different pLMs and, crucially, whether the designed proteins actually work in the lab.

The tension between what ENSEMBITS claims and how it actually performs reveals something important. The paper says it "matches or exceeds" static tokenizers on functional tasks like enzyme classification, Gene Ontology annotation, binding site prediction, and zero-shot mutation effects — but "matches or exceeds" is doing a lot of heavy lifting there. On some tasks it likely exceeds, on others it merely matches, and we don't know which ones. More critically, their RMSF prediction benchmark — where they actually outperform — measures how well the model predicts how much each protein region wiggles. That's a useful signal, but it's a single metric on a single type of prediction. When you broaden to functional tasks, the advantage shrinks to parity at best. That pattern — strong on a narrow benchmark, ambiguous on real-world utility — is exactly what made protein structure predictors feel revolutionary five years ago before the field had to confront how much harder functional prediction actually is.

ProtoMech has a different kind of fragility. They recover 82 to 89 percent of ESM2's original performance on protein family classification and function prediction, which means they're losing 11 to 18 percent of the model's capability. Then when they compress to circuits using less than one percent of the latent space, accuracy drops further to 79 percent of the original — a full 21 percent loss. Yet the paper frames this as a success because "steering along these circuits enables high-fitness protein design, surpassing baseline methods in more than 70% of cases." The 70 percent figure sounds impressive until you realize it means 30 percent of designed proteins failed to beat baselines. More importantly, "high-fitness" in this context is a computational prediction, not a experimentally validated binding affinity or enzymatic activity. We've seen countless papers where computational fitness scores correlate poorly with actual protein function once someone tries to express and test the designs. Until those ProtoMech-designed proteins are built and characterized, the 70 percent win rate is a number on a benchmark, not a proof of practical utility.

There's also an implicit contradiction between the two papers' assumptions about what matters. ENSEMBITS insists that capturing conformational dynamics is essential — that a tokenizer missing alternative states is fundamentally incomplete. ProtoMech, meanwhile, is extracting circuits from ESM2, a model that was pretrained on sequences alone without any explicit conformational ensemble information. If dynamics truly are as crucial as ENSEMBITS argues, then ProtoMech's circuits should be severely limited by the static training data that built ESM2 in the first place. Yet ProtoMech claims these circuits correspond to real structural and functional motifs including binding and signaling. Either ESM2 implicitly learned dynamics from sequence alone — which would undercut ENSEMBITS's core argument for ensemble-aware tokenizers — or ProtoMech is detecting something more abstract that merely correlates with structural features without truly capturing conformational flexibility. The papers don't address this at all, and neither provides the experimental data that would resolve it.
