The most striking piece of cleverness came from the way the new antibody database tackled the perennial leakage problem. If you’re pulling a sequence from SAbDab2 to train a model, first check the “ab‑ag‑split” label. When the same antigen appears in both training and test folds, the model will over‑estimate its ability to generalize to new epitopes. So the rule is: **if any antigen‑sequence identity exceeds 30 % between a training entry and a test entry, discard that test pair or move it to validation.** In the SAbDab2 release, 462 structures appear in both apo and holo states; those are the exact cases where the antigen is identical but the binding mode changes. Using them as a pure test of binding prediction will inflate performance, because the model can cheat by recognizing the shared variable region. When you see a test entry flagged as “holo‑only” but its variable‑region ID matches a training entry, treat it as a leakage warning and either remove it or re‑assign the split.

From the physics‑based design literature, a practical negative filter is **reject any designed sequence whose Rosetta energy gap between the target backbone and the lowest‑energy off‑target backbone exceeds 5 REU.** Historically designers focused on absolute total energy, but the review shows that large gaps flag designs that are only stable in the intended conformation because the scoring function over‑penalizes backbone strain. If you see a design with a total score of –350 REU but a gap of 12 REU, the rule tells you to discard it. The breakdown occurs when the target backbone is unusually flexible—e.g., a loop‑rich antibody CDR3—where the gap metric inflates due to conformational entropy; in that case, a smaller gap threshold (≈3 REU) is safer.
