Gaussian processes are the backbone of many modern machine learning models, especially when we need to encode prior knowledge about functions and perform Bayesian inference. But there's a catch: most GP tools assume the data lives on flat, Euclidean spaces. That assumption breaks down in many real-world scenarios—think of orientations in 3D, positions on a sphere, or transformations in robotics. These naturally live on curved spaces like Lie groups or their homogeneous spaces, and until now, building stationary Gaussian processes on such spaces has been a major challenge.

The paper tackles this by focusing on compact Lie groups and their homogeneous spaces—spaces where the group action is transitive and the space itself is compact, like spheres or rotation groups. The key insight is to use harmonic analysis on these groups. Specifically, the authors leverage the Peter-Weyl theorem, which generalizes Fourier analysis to compact groups. This allows them to represent stationary kernels as expansions in terms of matrix-valued basis functions, indexed by the irreducible representations of the group.

Here's why this matters: in Euclidean space, stationary kernels depend only on the distance between points. On a Lie group, "distance" is replaced by the group operation—so a stationary kernel is invariant under group translations. The authors show how to construct such kernels constructively, using the spectral representation provided by the Peter-Weyl theorem. This spectral form not only gives a principled way to define kernels but also makes it possible to sample from these processes efficiently.

The practical upshot is that these kernels can be plugged directly into standard Gaussian process software. The authors demonstrate this with examples on the circle (S¹), the rotation group SO(3), and the sphere (S²). For each, they provide explicit formulas for the kernels and show how to sample from the corresponding Gaussian processes.

One subtlety is that the spectral representation involves an infinite sum over irreducible representations. In practice, this sum must be truncated, and the authors discuss how to choose the truncation level to balance accuracy and computational cost.

This work opens the door to applying Gaussian processes in domains where the underlying geometry is non-Euclidean but highly structured—robotics, computer vision, and physical sciences, to name a few. By providing a constructive, computationally feasible framework, the authors bridge the gap between abstract harmonic analysis and practical machine learning tools. The available text does not provide details on the specific computational complexity or runtime benchmarks, but the methodology is clearly designed to be compatible with existing GP libraries, making it immediately useful for practitioners.

The paper demonstrates that this approach can effectively handle complex feature interactions and adapt to various scenarios, addressing the key limitations of existing methods. For question answering, MoDora uses a reasoning module that traverses the tree to locate relevant nodes, then synthesizes an answer by integrating information from multiple parts of the document. Mixtures of linear dynamical systems, or MoLDS, are a way to model time-series data where different trajectories can follow different underlying dynamics. Think of neural recordings where some neurons fire in one pattern while others follow a completely different rhythm. The challenge is that in real-world, noisy settings, standard MoLDS methods often struggle to separate these dynamics cleanly.

The paper tackles this by introducing a hybrid tensor-EM method. The core idea is to combine two complementary approaches: a tensor-based moment method for initial parameter estimation, and an expectation-maximization (EM) refinement step for fine-tuning. The tensor method works by computing empirical moments from the data—essentially, statistical summaries that capture higher-order correlations. These moments are then arranged into a tensor structure, which can be decomposed to recover the latent parameters of each dynamical system in the mixture.

Once these initial estimates are obtained, the EM algorithm takes over. EM iteratively refines the parameters by alternating between estimating the probability that each trajectory belongs to each system (the E-step), and updating the system parameters to best fit the weighted data (the M-step). This hybrid approach leverages the global consistency of tensor methods with the local accuracy of EM.

The authors validate their method on both synthetic and real neural datasets. On synthetic data, they show that their hybrid method outperforms purely moment-based or purely EM-based approaches, especially as noise levels increase. On neural data, they demonstrate that the recovered dynamical systems align with known functional groupings in the brain, suggesting that the method can uncover meaningful structure in complex recordings.

One limitation is that the method assumes linear dynamics within each system, which may not capture all types of neural activity. Also, the computational cost of tensor decomposition can be high for very large datasets. Still, the hybrid tensor-EM framework represents a significant step toward making MoLDS a practical tool for analyzing diverse, noisy time-series data.

Here's a deep-dive segment on the GABI framework:

When engineers need to understand what's happening inside a physical system - say, stress distribution in a bridge or temperature patterns in a turbine - they often face a fundamental challenge: they can only measure a few points, but need to know the full picture everywhere. This is the problem of Bayesian inversion, and it's usually highly ill-posed because you're trying to reconstruct a complete field from sparse, noisy observations.

The traditional approach requires knowing the underlying physics equations - the partial differential equations governing the system. But what if you don't have those equations, or they're too complex to solve efficiently? That's where the new GABI framework comes in.

GABI stands for Geometric Autoencoders for Bayesian Inversion. The key insight is to flip the traditional workflow on its head. Instead of learning a model after you've taken your measurements, you learn a geometry-aware generative model first, from data on multiple related physical systems with varying geometries. Think of it as building a library of how different shapes and structures behave under various conditions.

The framework works in two phases. First, you train a geometric autoencoder on a large dataset of simulations or measurements from systems with different geometries. This autoencoder learns a latent representation that captures how physical responses depend on geometry. Critically, this learned latent space defines your prior distribution - your initial beliefs about what patterns are plausible before seeing any measurements.

Then comes the "observe later" part. When you encounter a new system with a specific geometry and get some noisy observations, you use the pre-learned model as an informative prior for Bayesian inversion. The geometric autoencoder ensures that the reconstructed full-field solution respects the actual geometry of your new system, even if that exact geometry wasn't in your training data.

What makes this particularly powerful is that it works without requiring knowledge of the underlying PDEs or observation process. You're essentially transferring knowledge from similar systems to inform inference on your specific case. The framework enables full-field uncertainty quantification - not just a single best guess, but a probability distribution over all possible solutions consistent with your observations.

This "learn first, observe later" paradigm could be transformative for fields where collecting data is expensive or time-consuming, but where there are many related systems to learn from. The geometric awareness ensures the solutions make physical sense for each specific geometry, while the Bayesian framework provides the uncertainty quantification that engineers need for reliable decision-making.
