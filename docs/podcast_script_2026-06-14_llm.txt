What really set the “accidental taxonomist” study apart was the way the authors turned a hidden bias into a diagnostic tool. A naïve training run that just shuffles any two proteins into a negative pair would happily label a mouse‑human non‑interaction as “different species” and let the model win by learning that cue. When you pull a pair of embeddings from any protein language model, first check whether the two sequences share a taxonomic label. If more than 90 % of your positive training set comes from the same species, the model is probably learning “same‑species” rather than “interacts.” The accidental‑taxonomist paper showed a binary classifier built only on pLM vectors could separate same‑ versus different‑species pairs with 85 % accuracy. So, if your dataset is unbalanced, immediately re‑sample negatives to match the species of each positive. The warning sign that the rule has failed is a sudden drop in AUROC from ~0.95 to ~0.78 after species‑matched sampling; if the drop is far larger, you may have over‑pruned your negatives and lost useful signal.

If you are extracting embeddings for downstream design, never assume the default “last‑layer, mean‑pool” representation is optimal. The PEPE study demonstrated that swapping from the final transformer layer to the third‑from‑last and switching from mean to max pooling can improve downstream fold‑prediction AUPR by 7 %. A concrete heuristic: for any new PLM, benchmark at least three layer‑pooling combos on a small held‑out set of known structures before committing. The rule breaks down when the benchmark set is itself biased toward a single fold class; the warning sign is a near‑perfect correlation between embedding norm and sequence length, indicating pooling is re‑introducing trivial length cues.

When designing chemically modified siRNAs, treat each modification site as an independent categorical feature only if the surrounding dinucleotide context is represented in the training data.
