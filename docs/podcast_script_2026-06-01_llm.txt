What used to feel like a hard wall in our design toolbox is starting to crack open. In plant genomics, we’ve long relied on Hi‑C contact maps that are expensive to generate and then treated as static snapshots. The perspective piece on protein and RNA design reminds us that generative models are no longer limited to sequence‑only outputs. Together these papers tell us to stop treating structure, dynamics, and interaction data as isolated silos. The trick that made DynamicDTA stand out was the way the authors forced the model to see the same protein in many different conformations. Their question was simple: can a binding‑affinity predictor learn the contribution of protein flexibility, something static‑structure models completely miss? A naïve baseline would just feed a single AlphaFold2 model into a graph‑neural network and hope the learned weights capture motion. In practice that approach collapses when the ligand prefers a rare open state— the predictor would systematically underestimate affinity for compounds that bind only after a hinge bends. To avoid that collapse, the team generated an ensemble of 20 snapshots per target from short (5 ns) molecular‑dynamics runs, extracted per‑frame distance matrices, and encoded each frame as a separate node‑feature set. They then built a temporal graph where edges linked identical residues across frames, letting the network aggregate “dynamic fingerprints” before the final regression head.  

For validation they split the benchmark not by protein but by dynamic regime: the training set contained only proteins whose RMSD distribution stayed below 1.2 Å, while the test set deliberately included proteins that sampled motions above 2.5 Å. On the standard KIBA benchmark this split caused a 30 % drop in Pearson’s r for a static‑structure model, yet DynamicDTA retained a correlation of 0.71, essentially matching the static model’s performance on the easy proteins. They also ran an ablation where they shuffled the frame order, which erased the temporal edges and reduced r to 0.48, confirming that the cross‑frame connections were doing the heavy lifting.  

The cross‑chirality paper tackled a different kind of cleverness: designing D‑peptide binders for L‑proteins while the training data were almost entirely L‑peptide–L‑protein pairs. A naïve transfer would simply flip the chirality of the peptide sequence and hope the model generalizes, but the geometry of D‑peptides is mirrored, so the learned polar coordinates become meaningless. The authors injected axial vectors—explicit representations of the peptide’s handedness—into an E(3)‑equivariant polar network.
