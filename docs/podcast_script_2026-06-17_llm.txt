What really set the AlphaFold‑Multimer work apart was the way the authors turned template choice into a lever rather than a passive background. Their core question was simple: can we coax the same deep‑learning engine to spit out either the prefusion or the postfusion shape of a class I viral glycoprotein on demand? The cleverness lay in constructing four distinct template‑selection regimes and deliberately withholding the target’s own structure from the pool. By feeding only prefusion templates—say the influenza HA prefusion crystal—the model was forced into the high‑energy basin that matches that geometry, and the median RMSD to the experimental prefusion reference fell to 1.8 Å across the canonical benchmark. The authors split their benchmark of 12,000 protein‑ligand pairs into a scaffold‑aware test set where no protein backbone appeared in training, and a ligand‑novel set where all ligands were unseen. A naïve baseline that concatenated all features into a single vector collapsed on the scaffold‑aware split, inflating the Pearson r to 0.85 but actually memorizing protein families. By contrast, the heterogeneous GNN retained a robust r of 0.71 on the scaffold‑aware split and 0.68 on the ligand‑novel split, demonstrating genuine generalisation. Ablation of the evolutionary coupling edge type dropped performance by 0.09 r, confirming its contribution. As a sanity check they also ran a random‑graph control that shuffled edge connections, which plummeted r to 0.32, ruling out spurious correlations.

These tricks turn what could be a black‑box prediction into a rigorously tested, interpretable pipeline.
