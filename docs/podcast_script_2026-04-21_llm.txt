The Boltzmann machine learning approach in the Potts model paper connects directly to the statistical mechanics of protein evolution. The inverse Potts problem — estimating evolutionary fields and couplings from sequence alignments — is essentially a maximum entropy inference problem. The authors' use of persistent Markov chain Monte Carlo to sample from the Boltzmann distribution is a modern computational realization of this decades-old statistical principle.

What's particularly elegant here is how they're bridging two timescales: the evolutionary timescale captured in multiple sequence alignments and the physical timescale of protein folding. The pairwise couplings they estimate correspond to direct evolutionary constraints — not just correlated mutations but the actual physical contacts that stabilize the folded state. This is similar to the direct coupling analysis methods that emerged from the Marks and Onuchic groups, but with a more rigorous sampling scheme that avoids the equilibration problems of traditional MCMC.

The Boltzmann machine learning paper tackles a classic problem in protein design: how do you reliably estimate evolutionary couplings from sequence alignments when the data is noisy and the inverse Potts problem is computationally intractable? Their clever move was to replace the standard mean-field approximations with a persistent parallel Markov chain Monte Carlo sampler that runs continuously alongside the learning process. This means the model never has to burn in from scratch — it maintains a reservoir of high-probability sequence states that can be sampled instantly. A naive approach would use independent MCMC chains for each gradient step, which would waste most of the compute on thermalization and introduce high variance in the coupling estimates. Here, they show that their persistent chains converge in roughly 10% of the time while producing coupling matrices with correlation coefficients above 0.9 compared to the gold-standard pseudo-likelihood maximization on the same MSA.

The ConforNets paper faces a different beast: AlphaFold and its derivatives collapse to a single dominant conformation even when the native protein samples multiple states. Their solution is to train a latent diffusion model on known conformational ensembles, then condition OpenFold3's forward pass on these latents so the backbone can explore alternative states at inference time. The key methodological twist is that they don't just add noise — they learn a structured latent space where Euclidean distance corresponds to meaningful structural divergence. A naive noise-injection scheme would produce physically impossible geometries because it ignores the strong local dependencies in protein backbones. ConforNets instead uses a VAE pre-trained on 15,000 experimentally determined alternate conformations to ensure the diffusion samples lie on a realistic manifold. RosettaSearch takes yet another angle: instead of trying to predict a single optimal sequence, they frame protein design as a multi-objective search over sequence space using a large language model as the generator and RosettaFold3 as the evaluator. Their search algorithm maintains a population of candidate sequences and uses the LLM to propose mutations that greedily improve the multi-objective score.
