The diffusion model landscape is more unified than it appears, and that matters for protein design. A new timeline essay traces how today's sampling methods — DDPM, DDIM, score-based SDEs, and EDM — all descend from a small handful of foundational papers, each one solving a specific limitation of its predecessor. DDPM was slow because it used a Markovian forward process; DDIM fixed that with deterministic non-Markovian sampling; score-based SDEs unified everything in continuous time; EDM refined the variance schedules and introduced preconditioning. If you're choosing a diffusion model for antibody binder design, knowing this lineage tells you exactly what trade-off you're making: speed versus sample quality versus theoretical elegance.

On the interpretability front, Potts models refuse to disappear. These statistical mechanics frameworks from the 1930s, which model amino acids as discrete states in a Hamiltonian, are still providing insights that transformers can't. The key insight: self-attention in transformers is mathematically equivalent to solving the inverse Potts problem. A paper from Lupo et al. in 2022 showed that MSA row attentions actually outperform traditional Potts for contact prediction, but the hybrid approach — combining Potts-derived priors with transformer capacity — is where the frontier is moving. Caredda and Pagnani's 2025 work reduced parameter counts dramatically while improving interpretability. For a protein designer, this means you can have your neural network and your physics too.

The memory crisis in large language models just got more concrete. TurboQuant, released by Google, compresses the KV-cache during inference by keeping "outlier" channels at high precision while aggressively quantizing standard channels. The stakes are stark: OpenAI's Stargate project alone could consume 40% of global DRAM output. For computational protein design, this isn't abstract — it determines whether you'll be able to run a 70-billion parameter model on your own hardware in three years.

On the biology side, the "open window" myth is officially dead. The 2018 Campbell and Turner review in Frontiers in Immunology showed that post-exercise lymphocyte drops don't mean immune suppression — those cells are being redistributed to mucosal barriers by adrenaline, creating heightened surveillance. Athletes getting sick after hard workouts aren't experiencing immune collapse; they're experiencing non-infectious inflammation plus sleep debt.

Finally, a new BBSRC-funded sLoLa project is taking on one of biology's most elusive questions: how birds use quantum entanglement in magnetically sensitive proteins to navigate by Earth's magnetic field. The kickoff yesterday brought together experimental biochemists, molecular dynamics simulators, and machine learning specialists. Whether they crack the transduction mechanism or not, the project will produce insights into protein dynamics that will cascade into design.

The Potts model paper really takes us back to the foundations of statistical mechanics, and I think it's worth pausing on why this matters now. The maximum entropy framework that underlies how they learn couplings from MSAs traces directly back to Jaynes's work in the 1950s — the idea that the correct probability distribution is the one with maximum entropy given your constraints. When you have an MSA and you enforce that your model matches the observed single-site frequencies, the maximum entropy solution is exactly the Potts Hamiltonian. This isn't a coincidence; it's a deep principle. And what's fascinating is that the attention mechanism in transformers can apparently recover these couplings, which suggests something profound about what neural networks are implicitly learning — they're discovering statistical physical structure in biological data.

The diffusion models story follows a similar pattern. When you look at the SDE formulation from Song et al., you're really looking at a continuous-time version of what physicists have understood for over a century: the Fokker-Planck equation describes how probability densities diffuse through space. The score function is essentially the gradient of the log-probability, which is what you'd get from the force in a Langevin dynamics simulation. So these modern generative models are, in a sense, building on the same mathematics that describe Brownian motion and molecular dynamics — just turned inside out. Instead of simulating particles moving under a known potential, you're learning the potential from data and then generating samples by following the gradient of that learned log-probability.

This connects to something broader: the convergence of machine learning and statistical physics. Both frameworks deal with probability distributions over high-dimensional spaces, both care about energy landscapes and sampling, and both ultimately ask how complex behavior emerges from simple components. The quantum compass project, if it succeeds, would add another layer — showing that quantum mechanics itself might be harnessed by biology for navigation, which would extend this convergence all the way to the quantum realm.

The Potts model work offers something I find genuinely useful: a principled way to separate direct residue couplings from transitive correlations using Direct Coupling Analysis. The naive approach would be to simply count co-occurrence frequencies between positions in a multiple sequence alignment and call those contacts. But that fails catastrophically because if position A contacts B and B contacts C, you'll see a spurious signal between A and C even though they never touch. DCA solves this by inverting the covariance matrix, which mathematically isolates the direct interactions. The paper notes that this approach enabled the famous contact prediction results that preceded AlphaFold2, and it's still used as a baseline in ProteinGym for mutation effect prediction. That's the kind of method I'd steal — it's interpretable, grounded in physics, and still competitive.

The diffusion model timeline paper describes a different kind of cleverness: the progression from DDPM's slow sampling to DDIM's deterministic path, then to the continuous-time SDE formulation, and finally EDM's preconditioning tricks. What strikes me is how each method addressed a specific failure mode. DDPM worked but required thousands of steps — a naive implementation would be unusable. DDIM realized you could skip steps if you kept the forward process fixed but changed the reverse to non-Markovian. The score-based SDE unified disparate formulations under continuous time, which seems abstract until you realize it gave you the probability-flow ODE — a deterministic sampler with the same marginals as the stochastic version. EDM then fixed the practical problem that naive ODE solvers drift off distribution by preconditioning the network output and using a carefully tuned solver. Each paper essentially stress-tested the previous approach and found where it broke.

For TurboQuant, the method is the outlier channel identification — compressing the KV-cache uniformly would destroy model performance, but splitting into standard and outlier channels and compressing only the former preserves most of the signal. The paper doesn't give benchmark numbers in the source text, which is frustrating, but the conceptual approach is clean: not all attention heads matter equally, and you can identify which ones carry the critical information by looking at activation magnitudes. That's the kind of cheap, practical insight that works in real systems.

Here's a heuristic I find myself returning to again and again when I'm deep in a protein design project: **if your neural network mutation predictor doesn't beat a Potts model baseline, your architecture is overcomplicated for the task at hand**.

The Potts model paper makes this painfully clear. These are the same statistical mechanics models from 2011 that predict coevolutionary contacts from MSAs — they're interpretable, they have O(qN²) parameters that you can actually inspect, and they remain baselines in ProteinGym for mutation effect prediction. When Lupo et al. showed in 2022 that transformer MSA row attention could outperform Potts, they did it by capturing higher-order relationships, not by being fancier for fancier's sake. The heuristic isn't "never use transformers" — it's "if you're not capturing those higher-order relationships, you're just adding parameters without justification".

This matters directly in antibody design. Say you're training a model to predict escape mutations for a therapeutic antibody. You could grab a 1.2 billion parameter transformer and fine-tune it, or you could first run a Potts model on your target's MSA and see what coupling strengths it identifies. If your transformer doesn't improve meaningfully on which positions are coupled — if it's just learning the same patterns with more parameters — you've added inference cost without adding value. The failure case is obvious: your model performs great on training data but generalizes poorly to new variants, because it never learned the actual physical couplings, just statistical regularities in your dataset.

A second heuristic from the diffusion paper is more tactical: **if you're generating protein backbones and your sampler is slow, switch to DDIM-style deterministic sampling before you switch to a smaller model**. The whole point of the Song et al. 2021 work was that you can take the exact same trained network and sample dramatically faster by using non-Markovian forward processes — same marginal distributions, much larger step sizes. Before you retrain a smaller model or buy more GPUs, try swapping your sampler. The warning sign that you're in the failure case is if your samples look good but you're generating at 0.1 samples per second when you need 10 — that's a sampler problem, not a model problem.
