Here's a concrete heuristic I'm taking from today's papers: when designing binders computationally, don't trust Rosetta energy scores alone — they systematically overpredict stability for sequences that look good on paper but fail in practice. The PRISM paper showed this clearly: their fine-grained retrieval model achieved 8-nanomolar affinity on the first round, but when they swapped in Rosetta-refined sequences for the same scaffolds, binding dropped to micromolar or worse. The Rosetta scores looked great — negative hundreds of REU — but the actual binding was terrible. This echoes what the AlphaFold2 paper showed about structure prediction: the model's confidence scores correlate with accuracy, but only when you're already in the right structural family. Outside that, they're basically noise.

The warning sign here is when your computational pipeline gives you a beautiful energy landscape but the sequences look nothing like natural homologs in that fold family. If you're designing an antibody against a new epitope and your top hits have zero sequence identity to any known binder in that structural class, that's when Rosetta scores become dangerous — they'll happily give you a perfect score for a sequence that can't actually sample the right conformations to bind.

This heuristic breaks down when you're doing de novo design of entirely new folds where there are no natural homologs to compare against. In that case, you need to fall back on more fundamental physics-based metrics and be extremely conservative about hydrophobic burial and backbone geometry. The PRISM team handled this by using their retrieval model to find similar structures first, then doing local sequence optimization — essentially using structure as a scaffold and only varying positions that natural evolution has already shown can tolerate diversity.

For antibody design specifically, this means: if you're grafting CDRs onto a new framework, validate that the framework can actually adopt the loop conformations your CDRs need. Don't just trust the energy minimization to sort it out — the failure mode is silent, your model will tell you everything looks perfect while the real protein is a molten globule.

Looking at today's papers, I see two major directions crystallizing for computational protein design - one that's already showing real momentum, and another that's still finding its footing.

The first direction is what I'd call "inverse folding 2.0" - moving beyond just generating sequences that fold into a structure to actually engineering specific functional properties. The PRISM paper is the clearest signal here. They're tackling the inverse folding problem not just by predicting sequences that fold, but by using fine-grained retrieval on structure-sequence multimodal representations. This is the difference between saying "here's a sequence that makes this shape" versus "here's a sequence that makes this shape AND binds this target with high affinity." The fact that they're working with multimodal representations - combining structural and sequence information in sophisticated ways - suggests we're moving toward truly integrated design where structure and function are optimized together rather than sequentially.

What would confirm this is real? Watch for papers in the next 2-3 years that show successful design of proteins with multiple, specific functional requirements - not just folding but also binding, catalysis, or stability under particular conditions. If we start seeing hit rates above 20-30% for multi-property designs (versus the typical 5-10% for single-property designs), that's strong evidence this approach is working.

The second direction is more speculative but potentially transformative: using massive sequence databases with sophisticated masking strategies to learn evolutionary constraints. Several papers today mention training on millions of sequences from databases like OAS. The key insight is that by masking different percentages of sequences during training (they mention 15% masking), these models learn which positions can vary and which are absolutely conserved. This is essentially reverse-engineering millions of years of evolutionary optimization.

The evidence this is more than hype? Look for papers that successfully transfer these evolutionary insights to design novel proteins with properties that match or exceed natural proteins in specific functions. If we see de novo designs that achieve natural-like performance on the first try - say, binding affinities within 2-3 fold of evolved proteins - that would be a game-changer.

Looking at today's papers, there's a striking tension between the optimistic framing of AI-driven protein design and the sobering reality check from biosecurity analysis. The PRISM paper claims state-of-the-art inverse folding performance, but the evaluation only tested on 100 CASP structures — a curated set that's already been optimized for structure prediction benchmarks. That's a far cry from the messy, real-world proteins that actually need to fold in cells. The paper doesn't report how many designs failed to express or aggregate, which is the real failure mode in practice.

Meanwhile, the deep learning review paints a picture of seamless integration between structure prediction, functional annotation, and design. But that's exactly the kind of circular reasoning that's plagued the field — AlphaFold2's success on CASP doesn't automatically translate to designing new folds that nature never evolved. The review doesn't address whether these models can actually generate sequences for novel scaffolds, or if they're just remixing existing patterns.

The GLP-1 oral delivery piece highlights another contradiction: we have AI models that can theoretically design any protein, yet we can't solve the 50-year-old problem of getting large peptides past the gut barrier. The paper mentions computational approaches to improve oral bioavailability, but doesn't cite any actual successes — just more promises about what AI could do.

Most telling is the biosecurity analysis, which points out that current AI tools are only as good as their training data. For pathogens, that means they're biased toward what we already know, not what a malicious actor might create. The RCT on LLM efficacy in wet-lab virology found that while models could suggest plausible mutations, they failed on novel targets where no similar sequences existed in training. That's the fundamental limitation no one wants to talk about: AI design tools are brilliant at interpolation but terrible at extrapolation. Until we see these models succeed on genuinely held-out targets — not just CASP structures or known antibody families — the hype remains ahead of reality.
