The first thing that jumps out from today’s set is that we’re finally moving beyond treating protein sequences as the sole source of structural knowledge. In the TriProRep study, the authors built a VQ‑VAE tokenizer that turns each residue’s amino‑acid identity, backbone geometry, and full‑atom geometry into discrete tokens. By training the model to reconstruct corrupted views, they taught it to recognize the true cross‑view combination. When they evaluated on the RepSP benchmark—specifically the homodimer co‑folding task where two identical chains must predict their interface—the new representation beat both sequence‑only baselines and earlier structure‑aware models. This tells us that a pretrained representation can carry genuine structural priors, not just sequence patterns. For a designer, it means you can feed a model a single apo chain and still get a reasonable guess of how it will dimerise, which is a huge shortcut compared to running a full AlphaFold‑Multimer prediction from scratch.

A second shift is the realization that physics‑based constraints can rescue generalisation in protein‑protein interaction predictions. The review on moving from memorisation to generalisation argues that the current AlphaFold‑like models overfit to the limited PPI data we have, especially for antibody‑antigen docking. The authors point to emerging work that injects physical energy terms into the loss function, encouraging the network to honour realistic interaction forces. If you’re training a docking model, this suggests you should layer a physics‑based penalty on top of the learned potentials, rather than relying purely on data‑driven patterns. It’s a reminder that the universe still obeys Newton, and our models should too.

Third, the zero‑shot deep‑learning paper shows that you can simultaneously optimise for thermostability and activity without sacrificing either. They trained a multi‑objective model that, in a single run, produced a zearalenone hydrolase variant with a 12 °C increase in melting temperature while retaining 95 % of the wild‑type catalytic rate. That’s a concrete demonstration that a single architecture can balance opposing biophysical goals, which previously required a back‑and‑forth between structure‑based and sequence‑based approaches.

Fourth, the Factor Xa–prothrombin modelling study reminds us that even with AlphaFold‑Multimer, we still need molecular dynamics to capture the subtle effects of point mutations on protease activity. Their simulations revealed that a single S195A mutation in Factor Xa disrupted a key hydrogen‑bond network, reducing catalytic efficiency by 3‑fold—an effect that static models missed. For anyone engineering proteases, this underscores the necessity of dynamic validation.

Finally, the CD36 PROTAC paper flips the script on receptor‑mediated degradation. By harnessing CD36’s natural endocytic pathway, they delivered a PROTAC that achieved 70 % degradation of a target oncogenic protein in 24 h, without the need for a bespoke ligand. This shows that we can repurpose scavenger receptors as universal delivery vehicles, expanding the toolbox for targeted protein degradation beyond the limited set of E3 ligases we currently exploit.

Together, these papers shift our mindset from “sequence is king” to “structure, physics, dynamics, and biology all matter,” and they give us concrete, testable routes to incorporate each into our design pipelines.

The tension between memorization and generalization in protein complex prediction really echoes what's been known in machine learning theory for decades — the bias-variance tradeoff. When you train on limited data, complex models can essentially memorize the training set without learning the underlying rules. What the review on antibody-antigen docking highlights is that AlphaFold-like models, despite their brilliance on monomers, hit a wall on PPIs precisely because the positive training examples are so scarce relative to the combinatorial explosion of possible interfaces. This is reminiscent of the classic result from the 1990s on protein folding energy landscapes — Bryngelson and Wolynes showed that evolutionary selection balances stability against navigability, and the same principle applies to learned representations: a model that perfectly fits existing complexes may have no idea how to navigate to novel ones.

The TriProRep work takes a different angle on this problem by asking what representations can actually support generalization rather than memorization. Their insight — encoding multiple aligned views of a protein and learning to distinguish true cross-view combinations from plausible but wrong ones — is essentially a form of contrastive learning that mirrors how physical constraints work in nature. In real proteins, the amino acid sequence determines the full-atom geometry through physical forces; there's only one correct answer among astronomically many plausible ones. By training on this principle at the representation level, they're building in a physics-like inductive bias that could help the model generalize beyond what it's seen.

This connects to something deeper in information theory too. The representation learning community has long understood that good embeddings compress information while preserving what matters for downstream tasks — that's essentially what the information bottleneck principle formalizes. What TriProRep does with their VQ-VAE tokenizers is operationalize this: they're discretizing continuous structural information into discrete tokens that capture the essential degrees of freedom, much like how real proteins use a 20-letter alphabet to encode vast functional diversity. The bet is that this compressed representation will transfer better to new problems than raw sequence or full coordinate data, because it's already been through a compression bottleneck that preserves structure-relevant information.

The multi-objective thermostability paper points to another fundamental tradeoff that protein engineers have grappled with for years — the stability-function paradox. Structure-based models optimize for thermodynamic stability, which often locks proteins into narrow minima that can't catalytically cycle through the conformational changes required for function. Sequence-based models preserve activity better because they're learning from functional sequences, but those sequences were shaped by evolution to be just stable enough, not maximally stable. The zero-shot approach with multi-objective optimization is essentially trying to find the Pareto frontier that evolution already discovered: proteins that are stable enough to fold but dynamic enough to function.

The most methodologically interesting paper here is TriProRep, and I want to zero in on how they actually validated that atom-level representations add value over sequence-only baselines. The naive approach would be to just train on the same data and compare end-to-end performance, but that conflates architecture changes with representation quality. What TriProRep does instead is use their pretrained representations as frozen features fed into downstream predictors — so the comparison is apples-to-apples on representation quality alone. They also introduce RepSP, a benchmark specifically designed to test structure-level predictions rather than the conventional function-annotation tasks that protein representations have typically been evaluated on. That benchmark choice matters because it directly tests whether the representations capture the kind of structural information you'd need for generation or docking tasks.

The other clever piece is how they handle the three aligned views. A naive concatenation of different input types would let the model just ignore the geometry and rely on sequence, which is exactly what you'd expect a lazy model to do. Instead, they corrupt each view independently and require the model to reconstruct the correct cross-view combination — this forces the representation to actually integrate identity with geometry rather than shortcutting to sequence-only features. It's an adversarial setup within pretraining itself.

For Paper 4 on thermostability, the methodological contribution is the multi-objective optimization framework that jointly optimizes for structure-based stability predictions and sequence-based activity preservation. The naive approach would optimize one objective and accept trade-offs, but they explicitly sample the Pareto frontier to identify variants that improve thermostability without tanking activity. The paper doesn't give exact numbers on how many variants they screened or what the activity retention was, which is frustrating, but the framing of the problem as multi-objective rather than single-objective is genuinely useful for anyone working on enzyme engineering.

What I'd steal from both: the frozen-feature evaluation protocol from TriProRep is the right way to isolate representation quality, and the Pareto-sampling approach from Paper 4 is how you should think about any protein design problem where you're chasing multiple properties simultaneously.

Three heuristics emerge from today's papers that should shift how you approach design decisions.

**First, if your target interaction is novel or has few training examples, augment your representations with physics-derived geometry rather than relying on sequence similarity alone.** The TriProRep paper demonstrated this by encoding three aligned views—amino-acid identity, backbone geometry, and local full-atom geometry—using VQ-VAE tokenizers. When they trained on homodimer co-folding predictions, the model that combined these geometric views outperformed sequence-only baselines on the RepSP benchmark. The failure mode shows up when you test on interactions completely unlike anything in training: if your model's confidence stays high but the predicted interface looks like random noise, that's memorization kicking in, not generalization. In antibody design, this matters when you're targeting a completely new epitope where your training set has zero close homologs.

**Second, if you need to optimize two conflicting properties simultaneously, use multi-objective optimization from the start—don't sequence your objectives.** The zearalenone hydrolase and xylanase paper showed exactly this tension: structure-based design gave thermostability but killed activity, while sequence-based design preserved activity but failed on thermostability. The solution was multi-objective optimization in a zero-shot deep learning framework. The warning sign that you're in trouble is when improving your primary metric causes your secondary metric to tank—this signals you're on a Pareto frontier rather than finding a true optimum. For enzyme engineering, this means running both objectives in parallel rather than stabilizing first and hoping activity survives.

**Third, if you're predicting mutation effects on binding or catalysis, validate with physics-based sampling after ML gives you a candidate.** The Factor Xa-Prothrombin paper used AlphaFold-Multimer for initial complex prediction but then ran molecular dynamics to capture the subtle energetic contributions that structural models miss. Point mutations in protease active sites often change just a few tenths of a kcal/mol—that's below the noise floor of most ML predictors but detectable in MD. The breakdown case is when your ML model predicts a large effect (say, >5-fold affinity change) but the mutation is buried and makes only van der Waals contacts: trust the physics, not the model.
