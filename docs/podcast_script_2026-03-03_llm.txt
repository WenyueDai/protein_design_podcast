The legibility problem is what happens when AI systems start making scientific discoveries that humans simply cannot understand. It's not about AI being wrong or misleading—it's about AI being right in ways that exceed human cognitive capacity to interpret.

Think about how chess engines evolved. Early systems like Deep Blue played moves humans could follow and learn from. Grandmasters could study these games and improve their own play. But modern engines like AlphaZero make moves that seem bizarre to human observers. They're not wrong—they're just operating on a level of strategic thinking that humans can't access. We've gone from being partners with these systems to being passengers who trust the machine without understanding why it makes certain choices.

This pattern extends beyond chess. In science, we're already seeing AI systems generate hypotheses, design experiments, and analyze data in ways that produce results faster than traditional methods. But what happens when an AI discovers a new drug mechanism that works brilliantly, yet the underlying biology is so complex that no human researcher can explain why it works? Or when an AI proposes a materials design that's optimal but based on principles that contradict our current understanding of physics?

The core issue is that scientific knowledge isn't just about getting the right answer—it's about building a shared understanding that can be taught, critiqued, and built upon. Human institutions like peer review, replication studies, and educational systems all assume that knowledge can be translated into human-understandable terms. If AI systems start producing discoveries that can't be "translated," we risk creating a parallel scientific universe that exists alongside our own but remains fundamentally inaccessible.

This isn't just a technical problem—it's a philosophical and institutional one. How do we validate knowledge we can't comprehend? How do we teach concepts that exceed human cognitive limits? The available text does not provide details on specific solutions, but it suggests we may need new frameworks for scientific legitimacy that don't rely solely on human understanding. The question isn't whether AI will transform science—it's whether we'll be able to participate in the scientific revolution it creates.

Coherence Neuro is developing an implantable brain device called SOMA that treats cancer by delivering targeted electrical stimulation and recording tumor electrical activity. The company is starting with glioblastoma, the most aggressive form of brain cancer, and leveraging the biological intersection between cancer cells and the nervous system.

SOMA is an implantable brain-computer interface-like device placed in the skull during standard surgical procedures. It delivers localized electrical stimulation, including tumor-treating fields and other electrical patterns, directly to the tumor site. The device also records electrical activity from the tumor, creating a closed-loop system that can adapt its stimulation patterns based on real-time feedback.

This approach represents an evolution beyond Novocure's external Optune device, which uses alternating electric fields to disrupt cancer cell division. While Optune is effective, it requires patients to wear a bulky external device for 18 hours a day, limiting mobility and quality of life. SOMA aims to provide the same therapeutic benefits in a minimally invasive, implantable form factor that patients can live with more normally.

The biological rationale stems from growing evidence that cancer cells and neurons share similar electrical properties and signaling mechanisms. Both cell types use ion channels and electrical gradients to regulate growth, migration, and survival. By interfacing directly with tumors, SOMA can exploit these shared characteristics to disrupt cancer cell function while potentially enhancing the body's natural anti-tumor responses.

The device's ability to record tumor electrical activity is particularly novel. This creates opportunities for personalized treatment protocols, where stimulation patterns can be optimized for each patient's specific tumor electrophysiology. It also opens possibilities for early detection of tumor recurrence or treatment resistance.

Coherence Neuro's approach sits at the intersection of neurotechnology and oncology, two fields that have historically operated in separate domains. By bringing advanced neural interface technology to cancer treatment, the company aims to transform how we approach brain tumors and potentially other cancers that interact with the nervous system.

The available text does not provide details on clinical trial timelines, specific electrical parameters being tested, or the device's current development stage. However, the concept represents a significant shift toward more integrated, bioelectronic approaches to cancer therapy that could offer advantages in efficacy, convenience, and patient quality of life compared to existing treatments.
