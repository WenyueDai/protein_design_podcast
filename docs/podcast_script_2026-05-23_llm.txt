And when we rely on massive sequence‑only models for escape prediction, are we risking over‑fitting to prevalent lineages and missing rare, structurally constrained variants that never achieve high frequency?

The most non‑obvious insight is that sequence‑level representations can implicitly encode the dynamics needed to spot cryptic pockets, reducing the need for explicit structural ensembles. The most elegant idea is the symmetry‑driven nanocage: using a natural oligomeric scaffold to position heme‑like groups into a coordinated peroxidase‑mimetic active site without redesigning the chemistry itself.

Thinking about the pretraining idea from ProMoNet, I wonder how well it would hold up if we asked it to predict binding sites for proteins that have very few close homologs in the sequence databases it was trained on — would the performance drop sharply, or does the molecular foundation model side carry enough signal to rescue those cases? It would be useful to run an ablation where we strip away the protein‑level pretraining and keep only the ligand side, then measure the loss in cryptic‑pocket recovery on a set of targets where we know hidden pockets emerge only after large conformational shifts, like the allosteric site in SARS‑CoV‑2 main protease.  

The OMEGA assembly method promises low‑cost construction of hundreds of genes from oligo pools, but what is the actual error rate per assembled construct when we push the length beyond the typical 500‑bp range? If we could pair OMEGA with a barcode‑based sequencing readout, we could quantify how often frameshifts or mis‑ligations appear and then feed that error profile back into a design algorithm that penalizes problematic sequences before synthesis.  

On the MBP SNP front, the three rare variants — D173E, D173H and G176W — are flagged as deleterious because they sit in buried, conserved residues. A natural next step would be to express each mutant in a bacterial system, measure thermal shift assays to get ΔTm values, and see whether the predicted destabilization correlates with a loss of myelin‑binding affinity in vitro. If we find one mutant that barely affects stability but dramatically alters binding, that would point to a regulatory mechanism the pure stability predictors missed.  

The LSTM work on 3.1 million spike sequences shows promise for spotting escape routes, but we still don’t know how well it generalizes to a completely new virus, say a zoonotic coronavirus not represented in the training set. A concrete experiment would be to hold out all sequences from a particular clade — like the Omicron BA.5 lineage — during training, then test whether the model can still predict the observed rise in ACE2‑binding mutations when those sequences are fed in as a blind set.  

Finally, the EcBfr‑based nanocages with peroxidase‑like activity open a fun question about recyclability: how many cycles of dye degradation can they endure before the turnover number drops below 50 % of the fresh catalyst, and does that limit correlate with structural changes seen in low‑resolution SAXS after each round? Running a set of coarse‑grained simulations that track cage flexibility under oxidative stress could give us hypotheses to test experimentally.
